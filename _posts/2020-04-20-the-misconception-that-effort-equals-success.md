title: The Misconception That Effort Equals Success
description: A personal take on effort and the danger in conflating it with success in school

When I was going through high school, I thought I had the entire process of "education" and excelling academically down.

All I had to do was work hard. If I wasn't doing well in terms of grades or scores, it was because I wasn't working hard enough. I had to just keep grinding away, sacrificing everything that had the faintest chance of being perceived as detracting from my academic success. I kept time with my family to a minimum and anything that didn't have perceived educational value was considered a waste.

Keep in mind that these weren't conditions imposed on by my parents. I imposed them, enforced them, and followed them of my own accord. I yearned to be some kind of model son/student. Although my father never demanded too much from me and was always incredibly understanding, my mother was the exact opposite. I recall once getting a C- in algebra in junior high. She was absolutely livid and from that point on, I developed a fantasy of sorts where I'd be so successful she'd never have to scold or criticize me ever again. Both my parents would see that all the time and effort they invested in raising me would be worth it and I could be the pride of the family. 

This seemed to be the right approach to it all. I gained plenty of honors then, did well in my extracurriculars and managed to graduate in the top ten of my class. I gained praise from my teachers and did a great job of downplaying my exhaustion amongst friends and peers. I even managed to fool myself, seeing exhaustion as a sign of weakness and forcing myself into a more and more rigid mindset.

I knew it was a completely unsustainable way of life but I was certain that if I just stuck it out through college apps and college, I'd be fine. I'd pay with sweat and tears now, but I'd reign victorious at the end. I could reign supreme over any doubts my family had, and *become* one of the archetypal success stories my parents were so quick to flaunt to me ("Family friend *X's* son/daughter managed to get into *insert Ivy League here*").

My first wake up call came with college admissions. 

You bet I shot for the sky, I thought rather naively that although my standardized testing scores were not the best, they would see me for my individual merit. My essays would stand out enough and I could weasel my way in.

I remember quite clearly my dream school being either MIT, Caltech, or UC Berkeley. Something that my parents and family could say with pride.

I did not end up getting into those institutions. I remember feeling a deep sense of depression, a kind of shame that I had not lived up to my expectations or to that of the teachers who had put so much into helping me. This was immediately followed by pure frustration at myself. Surely it was just because I didn't work hard enough. If I had just not done *X* and allocated that towards studying....

I ended up accepting an offer to study Computer Science and Engineering (CSE) at UC Davis because of advice I had received from a former Green Beret Officer, Jim Gant. He had told me to take whichever route in life which maximized opportunity. I took that advice to heart and haven't forgotten it since.

UC Davis was far from what I had hoped for, but with the limited options I had it granted me the most opportunity in the future.

That's when my second wake up call came.

It's Fall Quarter of 2018 and I take my first Introduction to CS exam. 

Out of a possible 120 points I only manage to score 70. That's not even a 60%. 

I was furious with myself. I remember spending that day in total isolation. I just felt like I had let so many and so much down. 

It wouldn't be the only time I'd feel that way. There would be plenty other exams where I thought I'd given it all I could only to be "rewarded" with absolutely terrible scores. 

Why wasn't working harder just working out for me? Why did it feel like I was being punished rather than rewarded for the extra effort I invested? Why was it that my peers who invested significantly *less* time seemed to be doing better than me?

I knew this way towards success was obsolete. Dead. Broken and unsalvageable. Furthermore, if I kept thinking this way it would surely lead to my demise. This continuous unhappiness with myself would only lead to more complications in the future.

After much reflection I found my fatal flaw, beginning with the simple statement I used to try to console myself:

> Just because it takes more time for you to master something than your peers, does not *in any way* undermine how intelligent you are

From there things slowly fell into place.

Firstly, one has to acknowledge the fact that there are those destined to have an inherent advantage in certain subjects over others. For some individuals, Math comes a lot easier to them then say, English and for others, it can be the other way around. You can argue biology, environment, culture, etc. The list goes *ad infinitum*. The point is, there is a natural asymmetry in capability amongst all individuals.

It is something that I've always noted (at least, through my own observations), that the interests people pursue in their majors do not always align with what they are best at in terms of performance.

I found Communications and Economics to be trivial but I have no vested interest in them. I merely took them to satisfy major requirements. 

I do not doubt that there may exist an English major who would make a phenomenal Computer Scientist, or an Engineer who would make an excellent Biologist. 

Such is the nature of man, we do things not because they are easy, but because of the reward their difficulty may provide us. 

I digress, however. My point is that to **compete and compare oneself against someone with inherent advantage is an exercise in futility**, a child's game. One should gain what useful methodologies in success they have that are applicable to oneself but not to use them as the basis of your performance. They are to be respected, but not followed with such zeal as to harm how you see yourself.

Secondly, **each individual has their own unique way of learning**. I was cognizant of this, but not truly aware of its impact until I assumed my role as the Workshop Director for Quantum Computing at Davis (QCaD). 

In that role, I've had to develop all kinds of  materials to help break down a lot of the unintuitive math/physics into easily digestible content. 

As I went on with workshops and presenting these materials, I found that half the members would like all of my visual explanations and diagrams, while the other half preferred the more abstract mathematical explanations. 

Even within those halves, there were individuals who seemed to get stuck on different parts of a concept because of how they formulated mental models. 

The point is that **your way of learning something may be different from others, and that is not something to be ashamed of at all**. You are incredibly fortunate if you have an instructor that matches your way of learning but if not, there is no reason for panic. 

Finally, **scores are terrible way to compare people for intelligence**. Each individual is capable of very different things, things which cannot all be captured by any exam, assessment, or report. 

The reason GPA, letter grades, and test scores all exist is because we need some way to compare individuals quickly and efficiently. This is particularly true of our institutions of higher education which would rather prefer efficiency over thoroughness. 

Some argue that scores make evaluations fairer but I argue the exact opposite. To convert individuality into a mere series of digits and letters is an abhorrent and most repulsive practice. It is an evil we are complicit in only for the sake of efficiency, not for the sake of sincerity or genuineness.

Many a great mind have been labelled as slow, dim-witted and incapable of success only to be heralded as the geniuses and brilliant minds which our society desperately needs. 

So to the student reading this: 

> You are a unique, capable, and intelligent person. Just because your way of, or pace of, learning is different from your peers does not mean you are less intelligent in any way.

Sure, it might mean you'll have to put in a little more work than others, but there is no shame in that. 

I've been in your shoes and after this realization, I've found a new, more comfortable pair.

Hopefully, I've helped you found yours as well. 

Keep your head up, and go the only way you can go.

Forward. 

__*Semper Pregendum sine timore*__

"Always press on without fear"
